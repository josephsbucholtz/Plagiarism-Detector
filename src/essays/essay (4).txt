# On Love: The Force That Makes Us Human

There is a moment — perhaps you have felt it — when another person's presence becomes as necessary as air. It arrives without announcement, settles into the chest like warmth after cold, and rearranges everything quietly. This is love. Not the greeting-card version, trimmed in red foil and tied with a bow, but the real thing: ancient, complicated, and stubbornly resistant to full explanation. Love is the most written-about subject in human history, and yet no poem, no philosophy, no neuroscience paper has fully captured it. Perhaps that is precisely the point. To love is to encounter something larger than language.

## The Many Faces of Love

The ancient Greeks, who thought carefully about most things, recognized that love was not one emotion but many. They gave different names to its different faces. *Eros* was passionate, romantic love — the kind that sets the pulse racing and makes the world seem newly invented. *Philia* described the deep bond between friends, built on trust and shared experience over time. *Storge* named the natural affection between parents and children, the love that asks nothing in return. *Agape* pointed toward something even broader: an unconditional, selfless love extended to all of humanity. And *pragma*, perhaps the most underappreciated of them all, was the mature love that endures — not because it burns brightly, but because it has learned to sustain its own flame.

Modern life tends to collapse all of these into one word, and we are the poorer for it. When we speak of love, we rarely specify which kind we mean, and so we talk past each other. The teenager declaring love for the first time and the couple celebrating fifty years of marriage are describing experiences so different they barely share a category, and yet we give them the same name. This linguistic poverty shapes how we understand love — as a single, largely romantic phenomenon — when in truth it is an entire ecosystem.

## What Love Does to a Person

To fall in love is to undergo a kind of transformation. The psychologist Dorothy Tennov coined the term "limerence" to describe that early, overwhelming state of romantic love — the intrusive thoughts, the hope, the ache, the electric awareness of another person's existence. Neuroscience has since confirmed what poets always suspected: early love floods the brain with dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin in patterns that resemble, in some respects, both euphoria and obsession. We are, quite literally, altered by love.

But love's transformation goes deeper than chemistry. To love someone is to allow their existence to matter to yours. It is an act of profound vulnerability — an opening of the self to both joy and loss. The philosopher Simone Weil wrote that attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity. Love, at its finest, is radical attention. It is looking at another person — their flaws, their fears, their ordinary Tuesday-morning habits — and choosing to stay present with all of it. It asks us to be less self-enclosed, more permeable, more alive to the world outside our own heads.

## Love as Practice, Not Just Feeling

One of the most persistent and damaging myths about love is that it is something that simply happens to us — a weather event, a bolt from the sky. We speak of "falling" in love as though agency had nothing to do with it. And while attraction and chemistry are not entirely within our control, love as a sustained experience most certainly is. The psychologist Erich Fromm argued in *The Art of Loving* that love is not a feeling to be found, but a practice to be cultivated — like music or carpentry, it requires discipline, patience, and the willingness to improve.

This reframing is quietly revolutionary. If love is a practice, then it demands something of us. It asks that we develop the capacity to truly listen, to forgive without keeping score, to show up on ordinary days and not just the dramatic ones. It asks us to resist the cultural tendency to discard what no longer feels effortlessly exciting in favor of something new. Long-term love — the *pragma* the Greeks described — is not a lesser love than its early, intoxicating form. It is a more complex achievement. It has weathered disagreement, disappointment, and the inevitable discovery that no other person will ever be exactly what we imagined. And it has chosen to remain anyway.

## Love's Shadows

To speak honestly about love is to acknowledge its shadows. Love can be possessive, controlling, or weaponized into something that harms rather than heals. History is full of love used as justification for obsession, jealousy, and violence. The line between devotion and dependency is real, and it matters. Healthy love does not diminish the loved one; it does not mistake ownership for closeness. It enlarges both people rather than shrinking them.

Grief, too, is love's shadow — or perhaps its proof. To have loved deeply is to be capable of mourning deeply, and no amount of anticipatory philosophy fully softens that blow. Yet even grief is a testament. It means something was real enough to leave a mark.

## Why Love Matters

In a world that prizes productivity, efficiency, and measurable outcomes, love is radically impractical. It cannot be quantified, optimized, or put on a resumé. It resists the market logic that dominates modern life. And this, perhaps, is exactly why it matters so much. Love reminds us that we are not merely useful — that we have worth beyond what we produce or achieve. It insists on our humanity in the face of everything that would reduce us.

To love another person — in any of its many forms — is one of the few experiences that consistently points us beyond ourselves. It is an argument, made in feeling rather than words, that connection is worth the risk of loss. That presence matters. That the other person, sitting across from you right now, is a world unto themselves, and that world is worth knowing.

Love does not answer every question. But it may be the reason we keep asking them.
